BARANGAY CANTABACO

Cantabaco, with a land area of 10.14 square kilometers, Barangay Cantabaco is home to 5,665 residents. According to the legend, this barangay was unnamed until Blanca (who lived there with her husband Cadio and their two children) and her daughter went to the nearby river to wash their clothes. While they went about their task, they heard someone singing. Blanca thought at first that it was Cadio but later changed her mind after listening closely to the voice. Curious, they searched for the source of the voice and saw an agta-described to be a supernatural, big, black, and heavy man-singing (canta in the vernacular) while sitting on the lowest branch of the tree. He was smoking a tobacco leaf (tabeco in the vernacular). The name of the place today is the combination of canta and tabaco.

BARANGAY CAPTAIN CLAUDIO

Capt. Claudio, over a 10-year period, this barangay's population grew by more than 50%. Its area of 15.38 square kilometers is classified as agricultural. It is home to 3,908 people.

BARANGAY DUMLOG

Dumlog, According to the census, the area had a population of 2,669 in 2000. Its land area measures 2.16 square kilometers. The quiet barrio of Dumlog is situated on the other end of the Hinulawan river. It is noted for growing different varieties of bananas. Its name, however, is not culled from this distinct feature.

Rather, said the stories, Dumlog was named after the well-loved sport of the locals during the early Spanish era. The settlers then were fond of the sport similar to that of wrestling, which was called dunog. Accounts further tell that one day, there were Spanish conquistadores passing by the area. When they saw the locals playing dumog, they asked for the name of the place. Misunderstanding the question, they answered dumog. Its name today, Dumlog, is a corruption of the term.

BARANGAY GENERAL CLIMACO

General Climaco, malubog was the former name of this barangay and many people continue to call it after its old name. This barangay was named after one of the heroes of the Philippine-American War-General Juan Climaco. General Climaco was the Capitan Mayor or the town mayor of Toledo during the Philippine Revolution. After that, he was designated as the Jefe de Estado Mayor by General Emilio Aguinaldo. When the Philippine-American War broke, he served as one of the leaders of the Revolutionary Forces in Cebu, acting as its Chief of Staff.

Overpowered by the Americans' firepower and tactics, the revolutionaries were forced to make a last stand in the hills of Sudlon. Together with General Arcadio Maxilom, General Climaco led the Cebuano soldiers in a bloody encounter on January 8, 1900 that almost annihilated the local fighters. This led to the surrender of the troops one year later, on October 27, 1901, after which the Americans created a civil government. During the first elections, General Climaco became the first civil governor of Cebu. On his second term, he went to the US and assigned Sergio Osmeña, then the Provincial Fiscal of Cebu, as the acting civil governor. In the next elections, he "gave way" to Osmeña due to poor health. General Climaco passed away on July 16, 1907 at the age of 47.

There are 5,372 residents in this barangay, which has an area of 11.06 square kilometers. The story of the barangay's old name - "Malubog" - began when parents of a child reported that their daughter disappeared while washing clothes in the stream that traversed the area. The stream served as the "lifeblood" of the settlement, which was created way back during the Spanish occupation, providing settlers with water for farming as well as for their own consumption. Upon hearing of the girl's disappearance, the men of the village proceeded to the spot where she was last seen. There they saw that the water has turned muddy or lubog in the vernacular, as if its bottom had been disturbed by the motions of a big creature. Such sight then led to their belief that possibly a huge creature was living at the bottom of the pond.

To ascertain what was really living underneath the big hole, some men tried to go deeper into the hole and finally they saw a very big fresh water reptile which is known in the vernacular as bris. The people then called their place Malubog, which means "murky," alluding to the murkiness of the river during rainy season.

BARANGAY IBO

Ibo, today has a population of 2,796 and a land area of 3.11 square kilometers. In the old times, there used to be a big tree that grew in the sitio. It was so huge that it became the landmark of the area. The locals called this tree Ibo, and they named their barangay after it.

BARANGAY LANDAHAN

Landahan, the barangay has a land area of 4.17 square kilometers and 1,439 residents. In the early 20th century, when this place was still part of barangay Tubod, there was once a landed couple that lived there. They were Oyong and Onyang Samper. They grew coconuts in their vast tract of land. The couple had a very unusual way of drying their coconuts. While others used pugon, they laid out the coconut meat on several lantay, which are bedlike structures made of bamboo built for this purpose. Pieces of coconut meat were placed on top of the lantay, which were exposed to the sun for drying.

Because of the presence of the lantay, the locals started to refer to the place as lantayan. When this area eventually became a barangay, the name became Landahan.

BARANGAY LOAY

Loay, this barangay is the least densely populated in the city. Despite its 7.95 square kilometers of land area, it is home to only 965 people. Its population grew 17.54% over a period of ten years.

BARANGAY
MEDIA ONCE

Media Once, one of the big projects during the first years of the American occupation in the country was the construction of vital infrastructure. Roads and bridges were constructed all over the islands. In Cebu, a road connecting Toledo and Tabunok was one of the projects that was implemented. To complete the road, camps were built along the mountains that were to become part of the road. The camps were named Campo Uno, Campo Dos, Campo Tres, Campo Cuatro, Campo Cinco, Campo Seis, Campo Siete, Campo Ocho, Campo Nueve, Campo Diez, and Campo Once. Campo Once was the last campsite because what was supposed to be Campo Doce was very close to Toledo.

Campo Once was the last of the campsites constructed on a rolling plain, which is now sitio Bawud of Barangay Magdugo; midway between the last two campsites was Bahada, a gradual slope that ended in Patagan, Media Once is so- called because it is located midway between Campo Diez and Campo Once. The people agreed on the name. Now, the barangay covers 9.2 square kilometers and has a population of 5,377, according to the 2000 census.

BARANGAY POOG

BARANGAY POBLACION

Poblacion, Poblacion. The seat of Toledo City's government as well as the center of business activity, Poblacion is the second most densely populated barangay (next to DAS). It has a land area of 3.03 square kilometers, which is home to 12,823 residents.

BARANGAY SUBAYON

Subayon, this barangay has a population of 851 and a land area of 2.48 square kilometers. A long time ago, Subayon was sparsely populated. It was named after the narrow path that was the only access road from the barangay to the Poblacion. Subayon means to "follow a narrow strip of land or a trail that serves as a pathway."

BARANGAY TUBOD

Tubod, this barangay is called Tubod because water is so abundant here. It is said that when one digs even a shallow well, water will flow like a spring or tubod in Cebuano. Thus, this barangay is ideal for farming. Residents here plant rice, corn, and vegetables. As a matter of fact, due to the abundance of water here, Toledo City sources its water from this area. There are 2,316 residents in this 1.91-square kilometer barangay.

BARANGAY CABITO-ONAN

Cabito-onan, Barangay Cabitoonan has a population of 3,345. Its land area is 5.28 square kilometers. This barangay is so named because of the bito-on trees that abound, or used to abound, in the area. The fruits of the trees are shaped like that of the light bulb's filament. The locals then called these trees bito-on which is the Visayan word for "star". Cabito-onan is translated as "a place where there are plenty of bito-on trees." Few trees are still found in the area. Bito-on trees are useful to the locals who are mostly fishermen. The fruits are not eaten but serve as floats in the fishermen's nets. The trees also protect the residents' houses from strong winds.

BARANGAY CARMEN

Carmen, Carmen. In the place that is now called Carmen, there lived a wealthy couple - the Alburos. The wife, a Tagala, was very religious. She had a chapel built in honor of the Nuestra Señora del Carmen. This big chapel became the center of the religious life of the people in the area owing to the fact that it was the only one nearby. When the couple left the area, the residents decided to name their home after the lady's patron, Carmen. Today, Carmen is a barangay with 2,642 residents. It has a land area of 4.07 square kilometers.

BARANGAY DAANG-LUNGSOD

Daang-lungsod, When translated to English, this name means "old town". Technically, though, Daang-lungsod is not a town at present. It is only a barangay, which used to be a part of its neighboring barangay Sangi. Measuring 1.19 square kilometers, it has 2,855 residents. However, in the Spanish era, this relatively small village was once a thriving pueblo or town named Hinulawan. It was to be the beginning of what is now Toledo City. Hinulawan was bounded by the river of the same name.
In 1863, a very strong earthquake caused the walls protecting the Hinulawan river from overflowing to break. The earthquake was particularly devastating, causing floods and destroying the town. The residents of Hinulawan left their homes in search of a better place to live in. They settled in an elevated area, and more than a century later, this place is now the City of Toledo.

BARANGAY DON ANDRES SORIANO

Das, The barangay known as DAS is an acronym for Don Andres Soriano. It was formerly called P. del Rosario but people continue to refer to it as "Lutopan." Its name was changed to DAS after the death of Don Andres Soriano, the founder of Atlas Consolidated Mining and Development Corporation. The mining company has been operating in the city for more than 50 years and has been instrumental to the city's growth. Though the company suffered financial difficulties especially in the 1990s, Atlas continues to operate its mining and fertilizer companies in the city. The challenges faced by the company have resulted in the contraction of the barangay's population, which saw a decrease by 12.66%, from 21,592 in 1990 to 18,859 in 2000.

BARANGAY SANGI

Sangi, This is one of the smallest barangays in Toledo, with only a land area of 0.82 square kilometer. It is home to 3,553 people. Like many areas in Toledo City, this barangay used to be a quiet and rural area. All things changed when the Atlas Fertilizer Corporation opened there. What used to be a pastorał barangay was transformed into a busy one. In the vernacular, the word sangi mearts "junction point or area of convergence", which is what this place is. At the center of this barangay the three main routes or roads meet one going to Balamban, the other to the Toledo City proper, and the third to Cebu City. One description says: "The highway to Cebu City runs parallel with the Hinulawan river such that at the junction point of the three routes, the other two aforementioned routes branch out rowards the Poblacion and the municipality of Balamban, thus forming a Y-shaped direction." The uniqueness of the place's geography was the reason why Sangi was chosen as its name.

BARANGAY TALAVERA

Talavera, Originally, this barangay was called Calavera according to Toledo City's Registry of Deeds. However, this name was changed to Talavera. Seafood abounds in the coastal part of the barangay, making fishing a source of livelihood among its residents. Talavera became a parish in 1987. Its population is 4,916 and its land area is 6.47 square kilometers.

BARANGAY MATAB-ANG

Matab-ang, Matab-ang has a population of 6,706 and a land area of 11.11 square kilometers. Long before it got its name, Matab-ang was not populated because there was no source of water nearby. Residents had to walk for a kilometer to get water for drinking, bathing, and washing dishes. Fortunato, a fisherman who lived there, called for a meeting together with the other residents of the area. He proposed that they look for a source of water. The body decided to dig a well. However, since the area was near the seashore, many expressed their opposition to the proposal. Fortunato silenced their uncertainties by reciting this verse from the Bible "If ye shall ask anything in my name, I will do it". A religious person, Fortunato fully believed this, and therefore asked his friends to pray for their success. A well was dug following the prayers, and to their surprise the water was fresh, not salty. Thus, they called the place Matab-ang, meaning "not salty".

BARANGAY PUTING-BATO

Puting-Bato, There are 1,358 residents in this 5.9-square kilometer barangay. It was so named because there is a white rock or puting bato in the area that appears like a cliff. This is very unique so that people who are looking for it can really identify the rock. Puting Bato is accessible through sitio Suroy-Suroy.

BARANGAY SAM-ANG

Sam-Ang, The name Sam-ang is the old Bisaya name for burial ground. Dr. Felix Rodriguez, according to Atty. Cresencio Perolino, found a cave (called "Milagro" by residents) in the barangay where human remains were dug up. Perolino suggests that these are the oldest human remains found in the town of Toledo. During the Japanese occupation, this barangay became the hiding place of military and civilians. There were many ipil-ipil trees there (thus it was also called Caipilan then), making it a sanctuary for those who were seeking a safe place to stay. The thick foliage made it hard for the Japanese to spot the men and women in hiding. The place, again, served as burial ground during the World War II. There were 1,553 residents of the barangay as of 2000, according to the census. Its land area is 3.93 square kilometers.
