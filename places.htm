WORLD WAR II FOXHOLES
IN ILIHAN

Secret plans for the invasion of our country were devised, with knowledge of accessible points. The initial landing of the Japanese Imperial Army occurred in Lingayen Gulf, followed by a second wave in Davao, accompanied by successive bombings at strategic military installations. The outbreak of World War II on December 8, 1941, followed the sneak attack on Pearl Harbor in Honolulu, Hawaii, on December 7, 1941.

Long before the war erupted, individuals posing as businessmen, merchants, fishermen, and storeowners infiltrated the Philippines, masking their true identity as high-ranking officials of the Japanese Imperial Forces. Foxholes, serving as machine gun nests and shelters for USAFFE soldiers in the hills of Barangay Ilihan, were constructed in January 1942 after the Japanese invasion in Lingayen Gulf and Davao.

A pincer movement orchestrated by the Japanese High Command involved landings in Cebu and other Visayan islands. The USAFFE Battalion Commander of the 82nd Infantry Division MG Company, Captain Oligario Baura, led the defense at Ilihan, supported by officers like Lt. Bargayo, Lt. Florencio Blanco, Lt. Ramon Dumadag, Lt. Isidro Solijon, and Lt. Flores. Col. William F. Sharp, assigned by Let Gen. Douglas MacArthur, oversaw the Central Visayas division, later passing command to Let. Gen. Jonathan Wainwright.

Prior to the Toledo landing, Japanese Imperial Army scouts observed barbed wire entanglements along the shores, deeming it challenging to breach. Unable to overcome the obstacles, they landed in Pinamungahan on April 10, 1942. As they marched toward Toledo, clashes erupted with outnumbered USAFFE soldiers. Upon reaching the fortified defense line in the hills of Barangay Ilihan, intense fighting ensued. Despite the brave resistance by USAFFE soldiers, Japanese warplanes dropped incendiary bombs, emitting poison gas near foxhole openings.

At sunset, most USAFFE soldiers retreated to nearby mountains, planning to organize a guerrilla movement in cooperation with scattered soldiers. This resistance continued until the arrival of the Allied Liberation Forces, led by Gen. MacArthur. The battle witnessed heavy casualties among Japanese soldiers, particularly in the lowlands, due to fierce firing by USAFFE soldiers along the trenches. This chapter in Toledo's history stands as a significant testament to wartime struggles.

MALUBOG LAKE

This region of Toledo offers a complete package of natural wonders. From the enticing crystal-clear waters of Malubog Lake to the unique blue waters of Malubog Falls, and the captivating view of the Malubog Tunnel, it's a haven for those seeking exploration and awe-inspiring scenery.

Nestled in Barangay General Climaco, Malubog Lake and Dam are easily accessible with just a one-hour jeepney ride from Toledo's Poblacion Market. While the lake offers a tranquil setting for a leisurely day of picnicking and napping under a tree, observing locals engaged in fishing,

there isn't much else to do. For a closer view of the dam situated on the opposite side of the lake, you can either rent a boat for Php 500 or hop on a passenger boat, available on weekdays, for a minimal fee of Php 10 per person.
